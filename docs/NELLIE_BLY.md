# Nellie Bly: The Original Stunt Journalist

> "I would rather be a hammer than a nail."

---

## The Pioneer

Nellie Bly (born Elizabeth Jane Cochran, 1864-1922) is considered the pioneer of investigative journalism — and one of the first "stunt journalists" in America.

At a time when women were expected to write about fashion and recipes, Nellie Bly was exposing corruption, injustice, and abuse — often by going UNDERCOVER herself.

---

## Her Story

### Early Life
- Born in 1864 in Pennsylvania
- Started writing for the Pittsburgh Dispatch in 1885
- First major story: Exposing the terrible working conditions of shop girls — at age 21

### The Breakthrough: "Ten Days in a Mad-House" (1887)

In 1887, Bly went undercover as a mentally ill woman to expose the conditions at the Women's Lunatic Asylum on Blackwell's Island in New York.

**What she found:**
- Patients beaten by nurses
- Food described as "nauseous"
- Patients screaming and crying
- Poor medical care
- Overcrowding

**The Result:**
- Her exposé caused a grand jury investigation
- Six nurses were indicted for abuse
- The asylum improved its conditions

This was 70+ years before similar undercover work became common.

---

## Other Investigations

### Around the World in 72 Days (1889-1890)

Inspired by Jules Verne's "Around the World in 80 Days," Bly made the journey in just 72 days — setting a world record and becoming a celebrity.

### Exposing Police Corruption

Bly went undercover to expose police brutality and corruption in New York.

---

## Legacy

Nellie Bly is considered:
- The pioneer of undercover investigative journalism
- One of the first "stunt journalists"
- A precursor to gonzo journalism
- A feminist icon

Her work inspired generations of journalists who went undercover to expose the truth.
