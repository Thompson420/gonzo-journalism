# Nellie Bly - The Original Gonzo

## Full Biography

**Elizabeth Jane Cochran** (stage name Nellie Bly) was born on February 5, 1864, in Cochrane, Pennsylvania, and died on January 27, 1922, in New York City. She was a pioneering American journalist known for her investigative reporting and undercover work, considered one of the first gonzo journalists.

## Early Life

- Born in rural Pennsylvania
- 13 siblings (she was the 7th child)
- Father: Landed gentry, lost fortune
- Moved to Pittsburgh after father's death

## Career

### Early Work (1880s)
- Wrote for The Pittsburgh Dispatch
- First female reporter
- Covered divorce and women's issues

### New York World (1885-1898)
- Joined Joseph Pulitzer's New York World
- Became famous for "stunt journalism"

## Landmark Investigations

### 1. Ten Days in a Mad-House (1887)
**The Story:**
- Pretended to be insane
- Got committed to a Women's Lunatic Asylum in Blackwell's Island
- Exposed:
  - Patient abuse
  -atrocious conditions
  - Overcrowding
  - Force feeding
- Result: Investigation launched, reforms made

### 2. Around the World in 72 Days (1889-1890)
**The Journey:**
- Inspired by Jules Verne's novel
- Traveled solo around the world
- 72 days, 6 hours, 11 minutes
- Beat Phileas Fogg's record
- Carried a Siamese cat!

### 3. The Immigration Investigation
**The Story:**
- Exposed corruption at Ellis Island
- Documented immigrant suffering
- Helped pass immigration reforms

### 4. The Brute Force in Asylums
- Posed as pregnant woman
- Exposed treatment of expectant mothers
- Put "fallen women" in asylum

### 5. Around the World (1890)
- First solo female circumnavigation
- "Nellie Bly" became a household name

## Methodology

### Gonzo Techniques
1. **Undercover immersion** - Pretended to be someone else
2. **First-person narrative** - Wrote from personal experience
3. **Social justice focus** - Exposed injustice
4. **Dramatic storytelling** - Made issues readable
5. **Personal risk** - Put herself in danger

## Later Career

### Marriage and Retirement (1895-1922)
- Married Robert Seaman (steel magnate)
- Retired from journalism
- Adopted a daughter: Ruth
- Went bankrupt after husband's death
- Returned to journalism during WWI

### Final Years
- Worked for the New York Journal
- Covered women's rights
- Died of pneumonia in 1922

## Awards & Recognition

- First female Pulitzer Prize winner (nominee)
- Inducted into Hall of Fame
- NYC subway car named after her
- Asteriod: 6354 Navka

## Famous Quotes

> "I would rather have the stigma of having been a little crazy, than the reputation of being a sneak."

> "I am not afraid of the pen, or the scaffold, or the sword."

> "People may not have exact ideas of how things are done, but if you give them what they want, they'll read."

## Legacy

- Pioneered investigative journalism
- First undercover reporter
- Influenced generations of journalists
- "Stunt journalism" pioneer
- Gonzo journalism forerunner
